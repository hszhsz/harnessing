# Appendix A: Quantified Timeline of Human Energy Utilization

## Introduction

The story of human civilization is, at its core, a story of energy capture. From the moment our ancestors first controlled fire on the African savanna, each leap in productivity has been underwritten by a corresponding leap in the amount of energy an individual could command. This appendix presents a quantified timeline spanning roughly two million years of human energy history, tracking the per-capita power available to individuals at each major inflection point. The figures draw on archaeological evidence, historical engineering records, and modern energy statistics, synthesized from the work of Vaclav Smil, Ian Morris, and others cited in the main text. Where direct measurements are unavailable, estimates are derived from caloric intake data, mechanical output of contemporary tools, and thermodynamic first-principles calculations. Efficiency figures represent the best-available technology of each era rather than population averages. Readers should treat the earliest entries as order-of-magnitude estimates, with precision improving considerably from the Industrial Revolution onward. The timeline reveals a striking pattern: for most of human history, available power grew slowly, punctuated by sudden jumps that correspond to the "harnessing events" discussed in the main chapters.

## Notes

- **Per-capita available power** refers to the total primary energy flux accessible to an average individual in the most advanced societies of each era, divided across all end uses (thermal, mechanical, electrical). It is not the same as useful work output.
- **Efficiency** refers to the best-available conversion technology of the era, not the thermodynamic average across all energy use.
- Figures for the pre-industrial period are necessarily approximate and drawn from archaeological proxies.
- The dramatic acceleration after 1700 CE reflects the "Great Divergence" discussed in Part III of the main text.
- Modern figures (post-1950) are based on OECD-average primary energy consumption data from the IEA.
