# Part II — The Age of Natural Forces

*(~3500 BCE – 1760s)*

---

> *"The river had been running for ten million years. One day, a miller noticed."*

For most of the Age of Muscle, every joule of useful work on the planet had to be paid for in food. To plow a field was, ultimately, to burn calories. To grind grain was to burn calories grinding grain that would, in turn, become calories. The whole productive economy was a closed loop, bounded on every side by what a body could eat and how much that body could lift.

Then, somewhere along the great river valleys of the early civilizations, a quiet shift began. People started to notice that the world around them was *already* doing work — water was falling, wind was blowing, currents were running — and that none of it was being collected. A wheel placed across a stream could capture a thin slice of that motion and convert it, with no caloric cost at all, into the turning of a millstone or the lifting of a hammer. A sail could catch a wind that would otherwise have done nothing but rustle a few leaves, and translate it into the crossing of an ocean. For the first time, humans began to harness energy that the Earth was producing on its own account.

That is half of what this Part is about. The other half is even stranger, and it concerns a force that no one had previously thought of as a force at all: *information*. With the invention of writing, and far later with the invention of movable type, humans gained the ability to fix thought in a portable medium — to harness it, to store it, to transmit it across centuries and continents. A field plowed by an ox feeds a village for a season. A book carries an idea for a thousand years.

Three chapters carry the story. **Chapter 5 — Wind and Water** traces the slow spread of the waterwheel and the windmill, and the quiet economic revolution they made possible long before any factory chimney was ever lit. **Chapter 6 — Writing and Printing** follows the parallel revolution in information, from the clay tablets of Sumer to Gutenberg's press, and shows why the ability to mass-produce a page is itself a form of harnessing as profound as any mill. **Chapter 7 — Navigation and Gunpowder** brings the two together: the ships that crossed oceans on captured wind, guided by paper instruments and powered, when needed, by the controlled combustion of black powder, knit the planet for the first time into a single connected economy.

By the end of this Age, the world is no longer running purely on what its inhabitants can eat. It is running, increasingly, on what its inhabitants can *catch*.
