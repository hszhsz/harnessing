# Chapter 9: Internal Combustion and Oil — Portable Explosive Power

## A Farmer's Morning in 1900

In 1900, a wheat farmer in Kansas named William Saunders rose before dawn, as his father and grandfather had before him. He hitched a team of four horses to his harvester and spent the day cutting twenty acres — a good day's work. His horses consumed feed grown on a third of his land. He employed two hired hands during harvest season. At year's end, the food William grew fed his family and roughly four others. This ratio — one farmer feeding four to five people — had barely changed since the Middle Ages.

His grandson, Robert, farmed the same land in 1960. Robert climbed into the cab of a diesel-powered combine harvester and cut two hundred acres in a day. He worked alone. His tractor ran on fuel that cost a fraction of what horse feed had cost per unit of work. The food Robert grew fed not five people but over forty. Between William and Robert lay sixty years and one transformative technology: the internal combustion engine burning petroleum fuel.

This is the story of how explosive power became portable, how an ancient liquid extracted from deep rock became the king of fuels, and how the marriage of the two reshaped agriculture, transport, warfare, and the geometry of human settlement.

## The Principle: Controlled Explosions

A steam engine burns fuel outside the cylinder and uses the heat to boil water, which then pushes a piston. An internal combustion engine skips the middleman. It burns fuel inside the cylinder itself, and the expanding gases of combustion push the piston directly. This seemingly simple difference has enormous consequences.

The key innovation came in stages. In 1860, the Belgian engineer Jean Joseph Etienne Lenoir built a gas-fueled engine that operated on a two-stroke cycle. It was inefficient — about four percent thermal efficiency — but it worked. In 1876, the German engineer Nikolaus Otto perfected the four-stroke cycle that bears his name: intake (draw fuel-air mixture into the cylinder), compression (squeeze it into a small volume), power (ignite it; the explosion drives the piston down), exhaust (expel the spent gases). This cycle remains the operating principle of most gasoline engines today.

Otto's engine achieved roughly fourteen percent thermal efficiency — a major improvement over Lenoir's design. But the real genius of the four-stroke cycle was the compression step. By compressing the fuel-air mixture before ignition, Otto's engine extracted far more work from each explosion. This principle was pushed further by Rudolf Diesel, who in 1893 patented an engine that compressed air so violently that its temperature rose above the ignition point of fuel — eliminating the spark plug entirely. Diesel engines achieved twenty-five percent efficiency and above, approaching the practical limits suggested by Carnot's theorem.

Why does internal combustion surpass steam in power-to-weight ratio? Because it eliminates the boiler, the water, and the condenser — the heavy apparatus that external combustion requires. A steam locomotive might weigh a hundred tons and produce 2,000 horsepower. An internal combustion engine producing the same power could weigh one-tenth as much. This meant that for the first time, serious mechanical power could be made light enough to carry itself — and passengers and cargo along with it.

## Petroleum: The Perfect Fuel

Internal combustion engines can burn many fuels — gas, alcohol, hydrogen, coal dust. But petroleum-derived fuels proved overwhelmingly dominant, for reasons rooted in physics and geology.

Energy density is the critical metric. Gasoline contains approximately 46 megajoules per kilogram — roughly a hundred times the energy density of a fully charged modern lithium-ion battery, and about fifty percent more than coal per unit mass. Diesel fuel is slightly denser still. This means a vehicle can carry enough fuel for hundreds of miles of travel in a tank weighing a small fraction of the vehicle itself.

Petroleum is also liquid at ambient temperatures, which makes it trivially easy to pump, pipe, store, and meter into engines. Unlike coal, it requires no shoveling. Unlike gas, it requires no pressurized containers. Unlike wood, it does not rot. A barrel of crude oil is, in energy terms, one of the most convenient packages nature has ever produced.

The geology cooperated too. In the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, enormous oil reserves were discovered in Pennsylvania, Texas, California, Russia, and the Middle East. Production scaled rapidly because liquid petroleum flows under pressure from wells — unlike coal, which must be hacked from seams by human labor. By 1920, oil was cheaper per unit of energy than coal in most applications. By 1950, it had become the dominant energy source for the global economy.

The numbers are staggering. A single barrel of oil (42 gallons, or about 159 liters) contains energy equivalent to roughly 25,000 hours of human manual labor — over twelve years of work at eight hours per day. When you fill a car's fuel tank with fifteen gallons of gasoline, you are loading the energy equivalent of almost three years of a human worker's physical output. The cost? A few dozen dollars. This radical cheapness of concentrated, portable energy is the foundation of modern productivity.

## The Automobile: Reshaping Geography

Karl Benz patented his Motorwagen in 1886 — a three-wheeled vehicle powered by a one-cylinder gasoline engine producing about two-thirds of a horsepower. It was a curiosity. Within three decades, Henry Ford's assembly lines were producing a Model T every twenty-four seconds, and the automobile had begun reshaping the physical geography of civilization.

The productivity impact of motorized road transport is often underestimated because it is so pervasive as to be invisible. Before the automobile, every good consumed in a household had to be either produced locally or delivered to a railway station and then hauled by horse cart to its final destination. The "last mile" problem was not a metaphor — it was a literal economic bottleneck. Horse-drawn delivery in cities cost roughly five to ten times as much per ton-mile as railway transport.

Trucks eliminated this bottleneck. By the 1930s, motor freight could deliver goods door-to-door at a fraction of the cost of horse cartage, on flexible schedules, over any road. The rigid hub-and-spoke geography dictated by railway networks dissolved into a fluid web of point-to-point connections. Factories no longer needed to cluster along rail lines. Retailers could serve customers spread across suburbs. The entire economic geography of the twentieth century — suburban sprawl, shopping centers, industrial parks, highway corridors — is a consequence of the internal combustion engine's portable power.

The automobile also transformed labor markets. A worker with a car could commute thirty miles each way — ten times the distance practical on foot or by horse. This expanded the effective labor pool available to any employer from a radius of three miles to a radius of thirty, increasing the accessible workforce roughly a hundredfold by area. The matching of workers to jobs became more efficient, raising productivity even where no other technology changed.

## The Tractor: Feeding the World

Perhaps no application of internal combustion altered human life more profoundly than the farm tractor. The numbers are extraordinary and deserve careful attention.

In 1900, roughly forty percent of the American labor force worked on farms. Each farmer fed approximately seven people (including themselves). The work was relentless, governed by animal endurance and human stamina. Plowing, planting, cultivating, and harvesting a single acre of wheat required roughly fifty to sixty hours of human labor per year.

The first gasoline tractors appeared around 1900, but they were heavy, expensive, and unreliable. The breakthrough came with the Fordson tractor in 1917, which applied Ford's mass-production principles to bring the price within reach of ordinary farmers. By 1940, there were over 1.6 million tractors on American farms.

The tractor's productivity impact was twofold. First, it replaced horses and mules — and the land required to feed them. In 1900, the United States had over 21 million horses and mules, each consuming the output of roughly five acres. Those 100 million acres of "fuel cropland" were freed for food production or retired. Second, the tractor enabled much larger fields to be worked by fewer people. A single farmer on a tractor could plow in one day what took a team of horses several days.

By 1950, labor requirements for wheat had fallen to about five hours per acre — a tenfold reduction from 1900. One farmer could feed twenty-six people. By 1990, with larger tractors, combines, and chemical inputs, the figure was under two hours per acre, and one farmer fed nearly eighty people. Today, in the most mechanized operations, the ratio exceeds one to one hundred and fifty.

This agricultural revolution released hundreds of millions of people from the land to work in factories, offices, laboratories, and studios. It is no exaggeration to say that industrial civilization — cities, universities, hospitals, space programs — is built on the surplus created by internal combustion engines on farms. Without the tractor, modern urban society could not exist.

## Aircraft: Conquering the Third Dimension

The Wright brothers' first flight in 1903 was powered by a twelve-horsepower gasoline engine weighing about 180 pounds — a power-to-weight ratio that no steam engine could have approached. Internal combustion made powered flight possible, and petroleum fuel made it practical.

The productivity implications of aviation matured gradually. For passengers, the key metric is speed multiplied by capacity. A railway train in 1920 moved passengers at sixty miles per hour. A DC-3 aircraft in 1936 moved them at 170 miles per hour. A Boeing 707 in 1958 moved them at 550 miles per hour. The jet engine — a variant of internal combustion using continuous combustion rather than reciprocating pistons — compressed transcontinental travel from days to hours and transoceanic travel from weeks to hours.

For the global economy, aviation enabled just-in-time supply chains for high-value goods, made international business meetings routine rather than epic, and connected markets that geography had previously isolated. The productivity gains are difficult to quantify precisely but show up in the data: countries with better air connectivity have systematically higher GDP per capita, even controlling for other factors.

## The Geopolitics of Oil

Petroleum's extraordinary energy density and its geographic concentration created a new kind of strategic vulnerability. By the mid-twentieth century, industrial economies depended absolutely on a fuel that nature had deposited unevenly beneath a handful of regions — primarily the Middle East, Russia, and a few other areas.

This dependence shaped — some would say distorted — international politics throughout the twentieth century. Wars were fought over oil fields. Alliances were structured around supply security. The 1973 oil embargo, when Arab producers restricted exports, plunged the world economy into recession and demonstrated that productivity gains built on cheap energy could be reversed overnight when supply was disrupted.

The point here is not to rehearse geopolitical history but to underline a fundamental principle of harnessing: the more completely a civilization's productivity depends on a single energy source, the more vulnerable it becomes to disruption of that source. The internal combustion revolution delivered astonishing productivity gains, but it also created a systemic fragility that earlier, more distributed energy systems had not possessed.

## Quantifying the Transformation

Let us gather the numbers. Between 1900 and 1970 — the golden age of internal combustion and petroleum:

- Agricultural labor productivity in the United States increased roughly fifteenfold. One farmer went from feeding 7 people to feeding over 70.
- Freight transport costs fell by approximately seventy percent in real terms.
- Passenger travel speed increased fivefold for surface transport (automobile vs. horse) and thirtyfold for long distance (jet aircraft vs. steamship).
- Total energy consumption per capita in industrialized nations roughly tripled, while GDP per capita increased roughly fivefold — meaning energy efficiency improved substantially even as consumption grew.
- The share of the workforce in agriculture fell from over forty percent to under five percent in most industrialized nations, freeing hundreds of millions of workers for other pursuits.

The magnitude of these changes is difficult to overstate. In 1900, the fundamental rhythms of daily life — how far you could travel, how much food you could grow, how many goods you could move — had been only modestly altered from those of 1700. By 1970, every one of those constraints had been shattered by factors of ten or more.

## The Weight of Abundance

The internal combustion age brought abundance at a scale previously unimaginable. It also brought costs that its pioneers could not foresee.

Carbon dioxide emissions from petroleum combustion began accumulating in the atmosphere — slowly at first, then with accelerating force as the global vehicle fleet grew from a few thousand in 1900 to over one billion by the early twenty-first century. Urban air pollution from exhaust gases caused respiratory illness on a massive scale. Oil spills fouled coastlines. Refineries contaminated groundwater.

These costs were real but, for most of the twentieth century, invisible in economic accounting. The price of gasoline reflected the cost of extraction, refining, and distribution — not the cost of atmospheric disruption. This is not a condemnation of the engineers who built the system. They solved the problems before them with the knowledge available. But it is a reminder that harnessing always involves tradeoffs, and that some tradeoffs become visible only across generational timescales.

## Harnessing Moment

The internal combustion engine represents the moment when harnessed energy became truly portable — light enough to carry itself, dense enough to travel vast distances, flexible enough to go wherever roads (or skies) permitted. Steam had liberated production from geography; internal combustion liberated movement itself.

The marriage of internal combustion and petroleum created what might be called "bottled work." A gallon of gasoline is, functionally, five hundred hours of human labor sealed in a container you can carry in one hand. When that bottled work was poured into tractors, it freed humanity from the ten-thousand-year-old constraint that most people must farm so that a few may do other things. When it was poured into trucks and cars, it dissolved the tyranny of distance. When it was poured into aircraft, it made the planet small.

The harnessing pattern deepens here. In the steam age, humans unlocked stored energy from coal. In the petroleum age, they learned to package that energy in portable, precisely metered doses. The unit of harnessing shifted from the stationary engine bolted to a factory floor to the mobile engine riding in a chassis. Power went from being a place you traveled to — the mill, the mine — to being something you carried with you wherever you went. This portability of energy is what separates the twentieth century from all prior human experience.
