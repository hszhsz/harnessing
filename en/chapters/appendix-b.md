# Appendix B: Key Inventions and Their Productivity Multipliers

## Methodology Note

The productivity multipliers in this appendix represent estimated ratios of output-per-worker (or output-per-unit-time) achieved by the new technology compared to its immediate predecessor in the same task domain. Where possible, multipliers are derived from historical records, controlled archaeological experiments, or economic modeling. For ancient technologies, estimates draw on experimental archaeology (e.g., comparing harvest rates with sickles versus hand-gathering), while industrial-era figures rely on factory records and economic census data. Some entries represent compound effects across multiple tasks within a domain; these are noted with an asterisk (*). Multipliers should be read as approximate central estimates within often wide uncertainty ranges. A multiplier of "10x" means that a single worker equipped with the new technology could accomplish roughly ten times the output of a worker using the predecessor technology in the same time frame. The "domain of impact" column indicates the primary sector affected, though many inventions had cascading effects across multiple domains.

## Notes

- Entries marked with * indicate compound multipliers that aggregate effects across multiple sub-tasks within the domain.
- Multipliers are point estimates for the initial adoption period. Many technologies continued to improve after introduction (e.g., steam engines improved from ~1% to >40% efficiency over 200 years).
- The "predecessor replaced" column identifies the dominant prior technology in the same functional niche; in practice, transitions were gradual and overlapping.
- Several entries (particularly #24, Haber-Bosch, and #26, transistor) had civilizational-scale impacts that are difficult to capture in a single multiplier; the figures given focus on the narrow task domain of the invention itself.
