# Part IV — The Information Age

*(1940s – 2020s)*

---

> *"For ten thousand years we harnessed forces. Then, almost by accident, we began to harness thought."*

By the middle of the twentieth century, humanity had achieved something that would have astonished any earlier era: cheap, abundant, controllable energy. The steam engine, the combustion engine, and the electrical grid had between them lifted the per-capita power available to an ordinary person above what the wealthiest pharaoh had once commanded. In principle, the great productivity problem of the species — getting enough force to where it was needed, when it was needed — had been solved.

And yet, almost immediately, a new bottleneck appeared. Energy was plentiful, but *coordination* was not. A factory could produce a million parts a day, but only if someone calculated which parts to make. A bank could move money in milliseconds, but only if someone reconciled the ledgers. A military, a corporation, an airline, a city — each one was now an immense machine for converting energy into outcomes, and each one was choking on the symbolic labor required to keep itself organized. The world had more force than it knew what to do with, and not enough thinking to direct it.

The Information Age is the story of harnessing thinking itself. Not human thinking — that part remained stubbornly biological — but the symbolic substrate underneath it: the additions, the lookups, the comparisons, the routings, the bookkeeping, the millions of small logical operations that every coordinated activity quietly depends on. The electronic computer was the first machine in history whose output was not motion or heat or light but *decisions*. Once such a machine existed, the next two questions were inevitable. Could many of them be connected? And could a single person carry one in their pocket?

Three chapters tell the story. **Chapter 11 — The Computer** begins with the room-sized vacuum-tube machines of the 1940s and traces the long descent of computation from laboratory to desktop, as each generation of programming language and operating system widened the population of people who could put a computer to work. **Chapter 12 — The Internet** follows what happens when those machines start to talk to each other — when computation acquires reach, and reach acquires economy. **Chapter 13 — Mobile and Cloud** completes the arc, dissolving the computer into the air around us: a network of remote engines speaking to a slab of glass in the hand, available to billions of people in every country on Earth.

By the end of this Age, the substrate is ready. Energy is solved. Connectivity is solved. Computation is a utility. The ingredients are all on the table. The only remaining question — though hardly anyone phrases it this way at the time — is whether the machines that have learned to add, retrieve, and transmit can also be taught to *understand*. The answer arrives in the next Age, and arrives suddenly.
