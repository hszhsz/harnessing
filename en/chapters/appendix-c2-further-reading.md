# Appendix C-2: Further Reading

If this book stirred your curiosity — if you want to spend a weekend inside a particular era, follow a thread of evidence, or find a book that argues against our conclusions — this appendix is for you. The recommendations below are not the footnoted scholarly sources (those live in **Appendix C: Notes & Sources**); they are the next-stop reading list for the general reader who wants to keep going.

The selection principle, candidly stated: a sharp opinion beats a balanced summary. Each book carries a one-sentence recommendation with a point of view — what's good about it, what's annoying, whether it deserves your next weekend. Books are grouped by the five parts of *Harnessing*, with a final General section for works that cut across the whole arc.

---

## Part I: The Age of Muscle — How We Became the Tool-Using Ape

1. **Catching Fire: How Cooking Made Us Human**
   - Richard Wrangham
   - The most forceful rebuttal to the "hunting hypothesis": cooking, not hunting, packed the calories that grew our brains. You'll never look at a steaming pot the same way.

2. **The Human Advantage**
   - Suzana Herculano-Houzel
   - A neuroanatomist counts neurons across species and discovers that our advantage is metabolic, not structural — cooked food paid the brain's energy bill.

3. **Guns, Germs, and Steel**
   - Jared Diamond
   - The flagship of geographical determinism; the "why didn't the zebra get domesticated" chapter is the single most quotable piece in the genre — Chapter 3 of this book stands on its shoulders.

4. **After the Ice: A Global Human History 20,000–5000 BC**
   - Steven Mithen
   - A vivid archaeological tour of the Neolithic transition across every inhabited continent — the closest thing we have to a documentary on humanity's biggest pivot.

5. **Against the Grain: A Deep History of the Earliest States**
   - James C. Scott
   - The provocative counter-argument: early agriculture might have been a productivity *trap*, not a triumph. Read it to inoculate yourself against simple progress narratives.

6. **The Dawn of Everything**
   - David Graeber & David Wengrow
   - Picks a deliberate fight with the standard "complexity-equals-hierarchy" story. Ambitious, sometimes overreaching, but every page makes you rethink an assumption.

7. **Energy and Civilization: A History**
   - Vaclav Smil
   - The single most consequential reference behind this book — its Appendix A draws heavily on Smil's energy timelines. Read it slowly; almost every page changes a worldview.

## Part II: The Age of Natural Forces — Watermills, Sails, and the Not-So-Dark Middle Ages

1. **The Horse, the Wheel, and Language**
   - David W. Anthony
   - Traces horse domestication and the spread of Indo-European languages across the Eurasian steppe. Archaeology written like detective work.

2. **The Medieval Machine: The Industrial Revolution of the Middle Ages**
   - Jean Gimpel
   - A direct slap to the "Dark Ages" cliché: watermills, windmills, and mechanical clocks had been quietly laying industrial foundations for centuries before Watt — you just weren't taught it.

3. **The Great Divergence**
   - Kenneth Pomeranz
   - The defining debate question of economic history: why England and not the Yangtze Delta? Pomeranz's "coal plus colonies" answer is contestable but makes every alternative answer look thin.

4. **Science and Civilisation in China**
   - Joseph Needham
   - Don't try to read it cover to cover. But for any ancient technology — water-powered trip hammers, the magnetic compass, gunpowder — the relevant Needham volume remains the densest, most authoritative account in print.

5. **The Fall of Rome and the End of Civilization**
   - Bryan Ward-Perkins
   - Argues from potsherds and roof tiles that Rome's collapse really was a material regression — not the postmodernist's "it was just a different way of life."

6. **The Silk Roads**
   - Peter Frankopan
   - Recenters world history on Central Asia, with prose that reads like a novel. You'll finish it understanding why the cost of technology transfer is itself a productivity variable.

7. **The Measure of Civilization**
   - Ian Morris
   - A bold, almost reckless attempt to score 15,000 years of civilization on a single development index — the methodology is debatable, the ambition is admirable. One inspiration for this book's Appendix B.

## Part III: The Age of Heat — Steam, Iron, and the Century That Changed Everything

1. **The British Industrial Revolution in Global Perspective**
   - Robert C. Allen
   - Uses two prices — high wages and cheap coal — to crack open the puzzle of why Britain industrialized first. If you read only one book on the Industrial Revolution, read this one.

2. **The Unbound Prometheus**
   - David S. Landes
   - A 1960s classic still unsurpassed in its technical-historical treatment of steam, steel, and chemicals — but prepare to read a brick.

3. **Technological Revolutions and Financial Capital**
   - Carlota Perez
   - Threads steam, rail, electricity, and information into a single technoeconomic-paradigm cycle. For locating today's AI boom on a long timeline, this little book outperforms a year of tech blogs.

4. **The Most Powerful Idea in the World**
   - William Rosen
   - A narrative biography of the steam engine that emphasizes patent law and craft skill alongside thermodynamics — a useful corrective to lone-genius mythology.

5. **Empire of Cotton**
   - Sven Beckert
   - Tells the full violent supply chain of global capitalism through one commodity. Technological progress is never neutral; slave plantations were the other supply chain behind every spinning jenny.

6. **The Rise and Fall of American Growth**
   - Robert J. Gordon
   - Gordon's unsettling thesis: 1870–1970 was an "unrepeatable special century," and today's tech optimism may be badly miscalibrated. Read this and you'll be immune to a lot of Silicon Valley copy.

7. **The Railway Journey**
   - Wolfgang Schivelbusch
   - A strange hybrid of technical and cultural history about how the railway rewired human time, space, and nerves. You'll start noticing how each infrastructure revolution rewrote "normal."

8. **Coal: A Human History**
   - Barbara Freese
   - A more readable energy-determinism primer than *Empire of Cotton* — from Carboniferous swamps to climate science, told through one black rock.

## Part IV: The Information Age — Bits, Chips, and the Missing Productivity Boom

1. **The Information: A History, A Theory, A Flood**
   - James Gleick
   - From African drum languages to Shannon to quantum computing — Gleick is one of the rare writers who can make information theory feel like a thriller.

2. **Chip War**
   - Chris Miller
   - The most important tech book of 2022. How semiconductors became 21st-century oil and TSMC the center of geopolitics — read it and the news will read differently.

3. **The Second Machine Age**
   - Erik Brynjolfsson & Andrew McAfee
   - Confronts the Solow paradox head-on: computers everywhere except in the productivity statistics. The arguments have aged a bit optimistically, but the framing remains sharp.

4. **The Innovators**
   - Walter Isaacson
   - A collaborative biography of computing from Babbage to Google. Isaacson's gift is dissolving the lone-genius story into team biographies — the perfect Jobs-worship antidote.

5. **The Nature of Technology**
   - W. Brian Arthur
   - Treats technology as a self-creating, combinatorial evolutionary system. Theoretical, demanding, unavoidable if you want to take the "why innovation accelerates" question seriously.

6. **Scale**
   - Geoffrey West
   - From mice to elephants to cities to companies, power laws govern everything. Wildly ambitious — you'll start thinking in 3/4 exponents.

7. **The Wealth of Networks**
   - Yochai Benkler
   - How internet-era peer production challenges the firm-versus-market dichotomy. Slightly academic, but the best entry point for understanding open source, Wikipedia, and the gig economy.

8. **How the World Really Works**
   - Vaclav Smil
   - A sobering reminder that even the digital age sits on a hydrocarbon foundation — useful prophylactic against techno-utopianism.

## Part V: The Age of Agents — The Next Rein Hasn't Been Tightened Yet

1. **Genius Makers**
   - Cade Metz
   - How deep learning went from academic backwater to defining technology. Reportorial, story-dense — you'll come away knowing the personalities of Hinton, LeCun, and Bengio.

2. **Life 3.0**
   - Max Tegmark
   - Speculative AGI scenarios, more readable and more balanced than Bostrom's *Superintelligence*. Treat it as a thought-experiment collection, not a forecast.

3. **The Alignment Problem**
   - Brian Christian
   - A rare technical-ethics book that's both rigorous and readable. Read it before joining the AI safety debate on X.

4. **Human Compatible**
   - Stuart Russell
   - The senior AI textbook author makes his case for redesigning AI around uncertainty about human preferences. Quieter than Bostrom, more useful for working researchers.

5. **How to Avoid a Climate Disaster**
   - Bill Gates
   - An engineer's deconstruction of the net-zero transition; the "green premium" framing is genuinely useful for non-experts. Gates has visible portfolio biases — keep your salt shaker handy.

6. **A Thousand Brains**
   - Jeff Hawkins
   - A heterodox neocortical theory of intelligence. Hawkins is not a mainstream neuroscientist, but his core question — "how does the brain actually understand the world?" — should be on every AI researcher's reading list.

7. **Four Futures**
   - Peter Frase
   - When automation eliminates scarcity, what comes next? A thin book that sketches four scenarios (communism, socialism, rentism, exterminism) — the sharpest political-economic echo of this book's "harnessing" theme.

8. **The Future Is Faster Than You Think**
   - Peter Diamandis & Steven Kotler
   - The flagship of techno-optimism. You don't need to believe their timelines, but understanding the "converging exponentials" worldview is essential — it's Silicon Valley's operating system.

## General: Cross-Cutting Foundations

Books that don't fit any single part but will deepen your understanding of the whole argument.

1. **Why Nations Fail**
   - Daron Acemoglu & James Robinson
   - Institutions (not geography, not culture) decide national destinies. Pairs perfectly with this book's "technology plus institutions" frame — treat it as the missing institutional chapter.

2. **The Rational Optimist**
   - Matt Ridley
   - A bold case for exchange as the root of human prosperity. Pointed, combative, and the best counter-training against pessimism.

3. **Antifragile**
   - Nassim Nicholas Taleb
   - Taleb is always insufferable, always worth reading. Why some systems get stronger under stress — a genuinely original lens for thinking about how technological systems evolve.

4. **From Dawn to Decadence**
   - Jacques Barzun
   - When you've read too much technical history and need oxygen, this is the humanist tank. Barzun at ninety, writing five hundred years of Western culture like a long, lucid essay.

5. **Why the West Rules — For Now**
   - Ian Morris
   - A grand-narrative attempt to quantify East-vs-West development across millennia. Argues with everyone and worth arguing back with.

6. **The Structure of Scientific Revolutions**
   - Thomas S. Kuhn
   - Essential for understanding how paradigm shifts in knowledge translate into technological leaps — short, slippery, foundational.
