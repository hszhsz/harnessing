# Chapter 6: Writing and Printing — The First Information Explosion

## The Accountant's Dilemma

Around 3200 BCE, in the Sumerian city of Uruk — one of the world's first true cities, home to perhaps 40,000 people — a temple administrator faced a problem that no human being had ever solved. The temple collected grain taxes from hundreds of farmers, stored them in dozens of granaries, and redistributed them to workers, priests, and traders throughout the year. The quantities were too large, the transactions too numerous, and the time spans too long for any single mind to track reliably.

The solution he devised — pressing a reed stylus into a tablet of wet clay to make marks representing quantities and commodities — was not, in any artistic sense, writing. There was no grammar, no narrative, no poetry. It was accounting. The earliest known documents in human history are not epics or prayers but receipts, inventories, and tax records. They are spreadsheets.

Yet this humble, bureaucratic innovation would prove to be among the most consequential in all of human history. For what the temple administrator had invented was not merely a record-keeping technique. He had invented external memory — the ability to store information outside the human brain, in a form that persisted beyond the moment and could be consulted by others. He had broken, for the first time, the bandwidth limit of the human mind.

## The Bandwidth Problem

To understand why writing was a productivity revolution, we must first understand the constraint it released. The human brain is a magnificent organ, but it has hard limits. Working memory — the information we can actively manipulate at any given moment — holds roughly four to seven items. Long-term memory is vast but unreliable: it decays, distorts, and fabricates. And all of it dies with its owner.

In a pre-literate society, every piece of information — every law, every recipe, every trade agreement, every genealogy, every astronomical observation — must be stored in a living human brain and transmitted orally to another living human brain before its carrier dies. This creates several critical bottlenecks.

First, there is a storage limit: one person can only memorize so much. Professional memorizers existed in every oral culture — griots in West Africa, bards in Celtic lands, Brahmin reciters in India — but even the most prodigious memory has finite capacity. The Rigveda, one of the longest works transmitted orally, contains roughly 10,600 verses. Memorizing it was a full-time lifetime occupation. A modern university library contains the equivalent of millions of Rigvedas.

Second, there is a transmission bottleneck: information can only be passed from one mind to another at the speed of speech, to an audience limited by the range of the human voice. A teacher can instruct perhaps thirty students in a room. To reach a thousand people, the message must pass through intermediaries, each transmission introducing errors.

Third, there is a coordination limit: complex enterprises — taxation, law, engineering, military logistics — require that many people act on the same information. In an oral society, this demands either that everyone be present to hear the same speaker, or that messages be carried by runners whose memories may fail.

Writing shattered all three bottlenecks simultaneously.

## What Writing Made Possible

The effects of literacy on productivity are so pervasive that we struggle to see them, like fish trying to perceive water. But consider what becomes possible once information can be externalized.

**Cumulative knowledge.** Before writing, each generation largely started from scratch. Oral traditions preserved essential cultural knowledge but were conservative by nature — the effort of memorization favored stability over innovation. Writing allowed each generation to begin where the last left off. A mathematician could build on theorems proven a century earlier by a stranger in a distant city. Knowledge became cumulative rather than cyclical.

**Coordination at scale.** The great hydraulic civilizations of Egypt, Mesopotamia, and China — which organized the labor of millions to build irrigation systems, monuments, and defensive walls — were enabled by writing. Bureaucracies require records. Records require writing. Without the ability to issue written orders, maintain census rolls, and track tax payments, no state larger than a tribal confederation can function.

**Specialization and trade.** Written contracts, bills of lading, and letters of credit made commerce possible between strangers who would never meet. A merchant in Alexandria could place an order with a supplier in Muziris, on India's Malabar Coast, specifying quantities, prices, and delivery terms — all mediated by documents that outlived any single conversation.

**Error correction.** Oral transmission corrupts information systematically. Each retelling introduces small changes that accumulate over time — the mechanism that produces the infinite variation of myth and folklore. Written text, while not immune to copying errors, provides a stable reference against which claims can be checked. Science, in any rigorous sense, requires this stability.

## The Economics of Copying

For all its power, writing had a severe economic limitation: reproduction was expensive. Every copy of a text had to be made by hand, character by character, by a trained scribe. A skilled medieval European copyist could produce perhaps two to four pages of finished manuscript per day. A complete Bible — roughly 1,200 pages — represented six months to a year of dedicated labor by a single scribe.

This meant that books were extraordinarily expensive. In medieval Europe, a Bible might cost as much as a house. Even modest texts — a psalter, a book of hours — represented weeks of skilled labor. Libraries were small: a monastic library of 500 volumes was considered exceptional. The great library of the University of Paris in 1338 contained only 1,728 books. Information was precious not because it was rare in the world, but because its physical embodiment was costly to produce.

The intellectual consequences of this scarcity were profound. Knowledge was concentrated in institutions that could afford scribes — monasteries, royal courts, wealthy merchant houses. Access to information was access to power, and both were severely restricted. A peasant in thirteenth-century England had virtually no access to written knowledge of any kind. The information asymmetry between the literate elite and the illiterate majority was absolute.

## Gutenberg's Revolution

Sometime around 1440, in the city of Mainz on the Rhine, a goldsmith named Johannes Gutenberg combined several existing technologies into a system that would transform the world. He was not the first to print — the Chinese had printed from carved wooden blocks since the ninth century, and Korea had developed metal movable type in the thirteenth century. But Gutenberg's system — combining an oil-based ink, a modified wine press, and individually cast metal letters that could be composed, printed, disassembled, and recomposed — achieved something unprecedented: it made the reproduction of text cheap, fast, and scalable.

The numbers tell the story. A Gutenberg press could produce roughly 250 printed sheets per hour — with each sheet potentially holding multiple pages of text. A single pressman could produce in one day what a scribe required an entire year to copy. The capital investment was significant (Gutenberg himself went bankrupt), but the per-unit cost plummeted with volume. By 1500, printing houses across Europe had produced an estimated 20 million volumes — more books in fifty years than all the scribes of Europe had produced in the previous thousand.

The cost of a book fell by approximately 80 percent within the first few decades of printing. By the early sixteenth century, a printed Bible could be purchased for the equivalent of a few weeks' wages for a skilled worker — still not trivial, but accessible to a vastly larger portion of the population than ever before.

## The Productivity of Ideas

How do we quantify the productivity impact of printing? It is not enough to say that books became cheaper, though they did. The deeper effect was on the rate of intellectual production and the efficiency of intellectual work.

Consider a sixteenth-century physician. Before printing, to study medicine meant gaining access to one of the few manuscript copies of Galen or Avicenna — perhaps traveling hundreds of miles to a university that possessed one. With printing, a physician could own a personal library of dozens of medical texts, cross-reference them, annotate them, and build upon them. The productivity of intellectual work — the rate at which new knowledge could be produced from existing knowledge — increased by an order of magnitude or more.

We can see this acceleration in raw output. The number of distinct titles published in Europe grew from roughly 30,000 in the entire fifteenth century to over 300,000 in the sixteenth century, and over 600,000 in the seventeenth. This is not merely a reflection of cheaper reproduction; it represents a genuine acceleration in the rate of new knowledge creation, enabled by faster dissemination and broader participation.

The Scientific Revolution — that extraordinary cascade of discoveries between roughly 1550 and 1700 — is inseparable from this information infrastructure. Copernicus could read Ptolemy and identify his errors. Kepler could access Tycho Brahe's observations. Newton could stand on the shoulders of giants because those giants had published their work in printed books that could reach him in rural Lincolnshire. Without the printing press, the Scientific Revolution would not have happened — not in that form, not at that speed.

## Information as Infrastructure

There is a deeper lesson here about the nature of productivity. We tend to think of productive technology as physical — machines that cut, lift, grind, or transport. But information technology is equally productive, because coordination is itself a form of work.

Consider a simple example. A farmer who knows that wheat prices are high in a city fifty miles away can plant more wheat and sell it at a profit. A farmer who lacks this information may plant the wrong crop and go hungry. The difference in outcome is not due to any difference in physical effort or farming skill — it is due solely to information. The information itself is productive.

Writing and printing are infrastructure for this kind of productivity. They lower the cost of transmitting information across space and time, and in doing so, they enable more efficient coordination: better-matched supply and demand, faster error correction, more rapid innovation, and more effective governance.

This is why the modern concept of "intellectual property" — the idea that information itself has economic value — could not emerge until information could be physically embodied and distributed. A poem in the head of a bard belongs to no one and everyone. A poem in a printed book has an author, a publisher, a price, and a reader. The printing press did not merely reproduce texts; it created the information economy.

## The Democratic Tendency

Printing had a profound democratizing tendency, though it was neither immediate nor inevitable. By making knowledge physically accessible to a larger population, it undermined the monopoly of literate elites. The Protestant Reformation — whatever its theological merits — was fundamentally a challenge to the Catholic Church's information monopoly. Luther's argument was, at its core, that every Christian should be able to read the Bible personally rather than accepting the Church's interpretation. This argument was meaningless without printed Bibles in vernacular languages — and Luther's German Bible, published in 1534, sold hundreds of thousands of copies.

The expansion of literacy that followed printing reshaped economies. By 1800, roughly 60 percent of English adults could read — compared to perhaps 5 to 10 percent in 1400. This literate workforce was more productive not because reading and writing are intrinsically useful physical skills, but because they enable access to accumulated knowledge, participation in markets mediated by written contracts, and coordination in complex organizations.

The productivity gains from universal literacy are difficult to isolate econometrically, but economists have estimated that each additional year of schooling (itself dependent on textbooks, themselves dependent on printing) increases individual productivity by 8 to 13 percent. For an entire society transitioning from near-zero to majority literacy, the cumulative effect on economic output was transformative.

## Writing Systems as Technology

It is worth pausing to note that not all writing systems are equally efficient as information technology, and this had real economic consequences. Alphabetic scripts, which represent individual sounds with roughly 20 to 30 symbols, are radically easier to learn than logographic scripts like Chinese, which require memorizing thousands of distinct characters. The time investment needed to achieve full literacy in Chinese — roughly 6 to 8 years of intensive study — meant that literacy rates in China remained lower for longer than in alphabetic-writing societies, despite China's enormous cultural investment in education.

This is not a judgment of aesthetic or expressive quality — Chinese calligraphy is one of the world's great art forms, and the Chinese literary tradition is among the richest. But as information technology — as a system for maximizing the throughput of knowledge through a population — the alphabet was more efficient. The Korean invention of Hangul in 1443, explicitly designed for ease of learning, was a conscious recognition of this principle: King Sejong's promulgation stated that the script was created so that "all people may easily learn it and use it in daily life."

## The Compounding Effect

Writing and printing exemplify a unique feature of information technology: it compounds. A physical tool — a plow, a mill, a ship — produces output in proportion to its use. But a written idea, once published, can be read by a million people and trigger a million further ideas, each of which can be published in turn. Information is non-rivalrous: your reading of this book does not prevent another person from reading it simultaneously.

This means that the productivity return on investment in information technology follows a fundamentally different curve than investment in physical capital. A second plow doubles your plowing capacity. A second copy of a book does not double its informational content — but it doubles the number of minds that can engage with that content, and the potential for combinatorial innovation increases exponentially with the number of informed minds in a network.

This insight — that connecting minds through shared information generates increasing returns — is the same principle that drives the modern internet economy. Gutenberg's press was, in a very real sense, the first network technology: it connected minds across space and time in a way that generated value far exceeding the sum of its physical components.

## Harnessing Moment

The story of writing and printing is the story of harnessing information itself — treating knowledge not as an ethereal quality of individual minds, but as a resource that can be captured, stored, reproduced, and distributed through engineered systems.

The pattern of harnessing here differs from wind and water. With mills and ships, humans redirected physical energy that already existed in nature. With writing and printing, humans created an entirely new category of infrastructure — one that amplified not muscles but minds. The "force" being harnessed was the combinatorial power of human thought itself, constrained for millennia by the frailty of biological memory and the slowness of oral transmission.

The productivity implications were unique in another way: they compounded over time rather than diminishing. A watermill on a river produces the same output in its hundredth year as in its first. But a library grows. Each new book builds upon all previous books. Each new reader potentially generates new knowledge that enters the system. The printing press did not merely multiply a fixed stock of knowledge — it accelerated the rate at which new knowledge was created.

This chapter marks the moment when harnessing turned inward — when humanity began to harness not the external forces of nature, but the internal force of its own collective intelligence. Every database, every search engine, every AI model trained on the corpus of human writing descends from that Sumerian accountant pressing his stylus into wet clay, solving the simple problem of how to remember more than one mind can hold.
